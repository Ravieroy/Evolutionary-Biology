## 1. Shared Endogenous Retroviruses DNA[^7]
 Retroviruses reproduces by inserting itself directly into the cell's DNA and becomes a permanent part of the cell's genome.

| Retrovirus inserting itself into the host cell | On cell division the Retrovirus also gets replicated |
| ---------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| ![[20-retroviruses.png]]                       | ![[20b-retroviruses-copying.png]]                    |
**Retrovirus Insertion :** Normally retroviruses infect the white blood cells but if it affects the sperm cell or egg cell and end up fertilization then the resulting child will have a copy of the virus DNA in every one of the cell. It can be though of as the single giant mutation for the host.
It can have a negative effect but it is possible to have a positive effect [^1] [^2]as well.

When a retrovirus becomes a permanent part of DNA(functional or not) is called a Endogenous Retrovirus. 

==**Do we really know that these are really viruses?**== 
- YES, It has been shown that upon tweaking the virus to do what it was supposed to do, it started its original work to multiplying.[^3]

==**How likely is it that both species are affected by the same viruses in the same location?**==
- Very very unlikely because there are more than 10 million possible insertion spots in the human genome [^4][^5].
- If two individuals both are infected with the same 12 retroviruses, the chance that each of those inserted themselves in the exact same location in human and chimps chromosomes is less than 1 in $10^{25}$

### So what is the result?
- HERV-W Group is known to be common in humans and so researchers used this retrovirus. They found 211 insertions in human genome and 208 insertions in chimp genome. ==They found that 205 insertions were at exact same locations in both the host== [^6]. 
- If the fixed-species view were correct then the possibility of 205 insertions in the same location in both just by coincidence is 1 in $5.88 \times 10^{1418}$.
