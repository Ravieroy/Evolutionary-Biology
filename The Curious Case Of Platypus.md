- A creature with fur a bill and webbed feet that lays eggs and can secrete venom 
- Is this a mammal, a duck or some sort of furry reptile
- The platypus is classified as a mammal, specifically a monotreme, which is a group of egg-laying mammals that also includes the echidna.
- The platypus's egg-laying ability is thought to be a remnant from its reptilian ancestors, and its young are less reliant on egg protein than birds and reptiles.
- 340 million years ago the first amniotes appeared on earth in the form of small lizard-like creatures
- amniotes are four-legged vertebrates that are defined by the membrane or amnion that protects the embryo during development.
- Around 315 million years ago, amniotes split into two lineages: one leading to reptiles and birds, and the other to mammal-like reptiles.
- Eventually, most mammals evolved to carry their young internally, but **monotremes diverged about 200 million years ago**, keeping the egg-laying trait.
- Genetic studies show that platypuses retain the **vitellogenin gene**, crucial for producing egg yolk. While birds and reptiles have multiple copies of this gene, most mammals lost it. The platypus has retained some, explaining its egg-laying capability. But because they also nurse their young, the yolk is less essential than in birds or reptiles.
- Monotremes once thrived in ancient Australia but were mostly displaced by more efficient **marsupials** that migrated from South America via the Gondwana supercontinent around 70 million years ago. Only the **platypus and echidna** survived.
- One reason the platypus likely survived is its **semi-aquatic lifestyle**, which gave it an advantage over marsupials that couldn’t safely raise young in water due to their dependence on pouches. Platypus eggs can stay safely on land while adults forage in water, free from competition.
- The platypus’s bill is rubbery, soft, and incredibly sensitive its primary hunting tool. It’s both:

	- **Mechanoreceptive**: Detects pressure/touch via specialized cells called **push rods**, which react to vibrations and water movements.
    
	- **Electroreceptive**: Can detect weak electrical fields emitted by prey muscle movements. Its bill contains ~70,000 electroreceptors arranged in striped patterns alternating with mechanoreceptors.
- This **electrosensory capability** is rare in mammals and shared only with the echidna (in a limited way) and the Guiana dolphin. The echidna has few remaining electroreceptors, a sign of evolutionary regression as it became fully terrestrial.
- The platypus evolved **electroreception independently** from fish, showing **convergent evolution** different species developing similar traits to solve the same problem.
- Male platypuses have venomous spurs on their hind legs. the **platypus venom shares proteins with reptile venom**, despite 315 million years of evolutionary divergence. Genome sequencing shows that venom genes in the platypus and in reptiles underwent similar duplications independently, example of **convergent evolution**.
