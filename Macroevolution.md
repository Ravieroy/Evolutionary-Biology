## Definitions 

### Def. 1
> *Large evolutionary change, usually in morphology; typically refers to the evolution of differences among populations that would warrant their placement in different genera or higher-level taxa.*
> 
> Herron, Jon C. and Scott Freeman. 2014. _Evolutionary Analysis 5th edition._

### Def. 2
> *Macroevolution is evolution occurring above the species level, including the origination, diversification, and extinction of species over long periods of evolutionary time.*
> 
> Emlen, Douglas J. and Carl Zimmer. 2013. _Evolution: Making Sense of Life 3rd edition_.

### Def. 3
> *A vague term, usually meaning the evolution of substantial phenotypic changes, usually great enough to place the changed lineage and its descendants in a distinct genus or higher taxon.*
> 
> Futuyma, Douglas J. and Mark Kirkpatrick. 2017. _Evolution 4th edition._

### Def. 4
> *Macroevolution is evolution on a grand scale — what we see when we look at the over-arching history of life: stability, change, lineages arising, and extinction.*
> 
> [Macroevolution | Berkeley](https://evolution.berkeley.edu/evolution-101/macroevolution/)

### Explanation

Macroevolution generally refers to **evolution above the species level**. So instead of focusing on an individual [[Species]], a macroevolutionary lens might require that we zoom out on the tree of life, to assess the diversity of the entire [[Clade]] and its position on the tree.

Macroevolution can be described as an accumulations of [[Microevolution]], subsequently causing an individual to lose its ability to produce fertile offspring with other members of his original species.

**Does this mean that the gametes (XX, XY) must be affected by random mutations in order for a new species to emerge from another origin-species?**

No, the emergence of a new species can also be caused by non-gametical mutations happening, which have an effect on mating behavior which causes this individual to lose its ability to mate with members of his original species. Those could be changes which affect the physiology, preferred environment, mating times, etc.

## Important Points
1. Once a lineage has diverged into two or more distinct species, from that point on, all microevolutionary changes within each lineage will remain distinct and restricted to that lineage, and all its descendant lineages, only. 
2. Macroevolutionary changes are just lineage-restricted microevolutionary changes, and are thus basically a post-hoc designation we apply long after lineage divergence. It's  never big changes all at once, it's just slow, gradual drift.
3. Macroevolution and [[Microevolution]] has little to do with the appearance or the size of the change. i.e. Macroevolution doesn't mean a large change in appearance. 
4. Population can evolve to look very different but if they can produce viable off-spring , they are still the same species and those changes no matter how large are [[Microevolution]].
5. At the DNA level [[Microevolution]] and Macroevolution are exactly the same, it's how they manifest themselves sets them apart.
6. Two populations originally of the same [[Species]], if isolated long enough can accumulate small mutations([[Microevolution]]) and become genetically incompatible and evolve into separate [[Species]](Macroevolution).

## Causes of Macroevolution 
![[01b-macroevolution.png]]

Similar to [[Microevolution]], basic evolutionary mechanisms like mutation, migration, genetic drift, and natural selection are at work. However in the larger picture following are the causes:

1. Phyletic Gradualism : If we start with a common ancestor, there is a slow accumulation of changes over a long period of time before they diverge so much that they are a separate [[Species]]. There would be a lot of [[Transitional fossils]].
2. Punctuated Equilibrium : A quick change(in geological time scale like 30000 Years) due some environmental aspects or some factor. Due to quick change, there is lesser number of transitional fossils in this case.  
## Miscellaneous 
The simple fact of the matter is that macro-evolution as a construct EMERGED from hypothesis testing and data validation by scientists. The theory of natural selection gave scientists a series of predictions to test, and from that those being proven right and wrong, we have begun to understand how the process of evolution works.

For example:

- If macro-evolution is true, we should be able to see the development of our own species in the fossil record: proven true ([Read this](https://www.nature.com/scitable/knowledge/library/overview-of-hominin-evolution-89010983/))
    
- If macro-evolution is true, we should be able to see the emergence and disappearance of multiple evolutionary adaptions in species over the fossil record: proven true
    
- If macro-evolution is true, we should see a linear path of the complexity of life on the planet, associated with the geological record: proven true
