## 1. Scientists recreate mouse from gene older than animal life [^1]
![[Ref1.png|400]]

**Date :** Nov 18, 2024
**Ref. :** _Nature Communications_, 2024; 15 (1) DOI: [10.1038/s41467-024-54152-x](http://dx.doi.org/10.1038/s41467-024-54152-x)
**Summary :** An international team of researchers has achieved[^3] an unprecedented milestone: the ==creation of mouse stem cells capable of generating a fully developed mouse using genetic tools from a unicellular organism, with which we share a common ancestor that predates animals==. This breakthrough reshapes our understanding of the genetic origins of stem cells, offering a new perspective on the evolutionary ties between animals and their ancient single-celled relatives.
**Key Points**
1. "The ==study implies that key genes involved in stem cell formation might have originated far earlier than the stem cells themselves, perhaps helping pave the way for the multicellular life we see today.==" : Dr Alex de Mendoza
2. Team introduced choanoflagellate Sox genes into mouse cells, replacing the native Sox2 gene achieving reprogramming towards the pluripotent stem cell state.
3. These reprogrammed cells, they were injected into a developing mouse embryo.
4. The study traces how early versions of Sox and POU proteins, which bind DNA and regulate other genes, were used by unicellular ancestors for functions that would later become integral to stem cell formation and animal development.
5. "_Choanoflagellates_ don't have stem cells, they're single-celled organisms, ==but they have these genes, likely to control basic cellular processes that multicellular animals probably later repurposed for building complex bodies==,"
6. Unicellular organisms must have omnipotent cells. Stem cells are merely pluripotent, and adding that gene from the omnipotent unicellular organisms returns specialized cells to a pluripotent state.


## 2. Genome Of Marine Organism Tells Of Humans' Unicellular Ancestors [^4]
**Date :** Feb 20, 2008
**Summary :** A ubiquitous but little-known marine organism, ==the choanoflagellate, is the last one-celled ancestor of humans and provides insight into how cells learned to assemble into multicelled organisms.== The genome of the choanoflagellate Monisiga brevicollis has now been sequenced[^2] and offers clues to the origin of the glue that holds many-celled animals together and how cells learned to communicate.


## 3. Origins of multicellularity in response to predation [^5]
![[Ref2.png|400]]

**Date :** Feb 20, 2019
**Ref. :** [Scientific Reports volume 9, Article number: 2328 (2019)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-019-39558-8)
**Summary :** This study showed that simple multicellularity can evolve in response to predation.
Authors outcrossed populations of the unicellular green alga _Chlamydomonas reinhardtii_ to selection by the filter-feeding predator _Paramecium tetraurelia_. Two of five experimental populations evolved multicellular structures not observed in unselected control populations within ~750 asexual generations.

## 4. Statistical evidence for common ancestry: Application to primates [^6]
**Date :** May 03, 2016
**Ref. :** [_Evolution_, Volume 70, Issue 6, 1 June 2016, Pages 1354–1363](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/evo.12934)
**Summary :** This study explore a diversity of statistical methods using data from the primates focusing on alternatives to common ancestry(CA) namely species separate ancestry(SA) (the separate origin of each named species) and family SA (the separate origin of each family). They overwhelmingly rejected both species and family SA with infinitesimal _P_ values. Comparing these results they found tremendously strong support for the CA of all primates.

## 5. Development of multicellularity from unicellularity under laboratory conditions [^7]
![[Ref3.png|400]]

**Date :** Apr 4, 2023
**Ref.:** _Proc. Natl. Acad. Sci. U.S.A._ 120 (18) e2216668120,
**Summary :** Discovered that aggregation of a unicellular close animal relative is induced by chemical cues, as observed in some animals and other unicellular species. This discovery expands the prevalence of chemically regulated multicellularity in unicellular animal relatives, which suggests that this trait may have been common in the close unicellular ancestors of animals.
